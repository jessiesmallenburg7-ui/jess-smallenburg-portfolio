Project Overview

“How might we”

How might we redesign EHR clinical data review and documentation so primary care clinicians can manage charting with less cognitive load, reclaim time for patients, and produce trustworthy plain-language updates that help family caregivers stay informed?

Primary care clinicians often spend more time in the EHR than with patients—feeding burnout and eroding care quality.

AI documentation tools promise speed, but they introduce new risks: hallucination, opaque sourcing, and unclear verification paths. Social determinants of health (SDOH) stay buried across screens. Caregivers get jargon-heavy fragments instead of answers they can act on between visits.

Lumen Chart explores how clearer interface design, AI provenance, and contextual patient data can reduce clinician cognitive load while making information more usable for caregivers.

High-Level Outcomes & Impact

Intended outcomes:

  • Faster chart synthesis

    Cut pre-visit review from 3–5 minutes to under 60 seconds with a unified AI-supported snapshot.

  • Less documentation friction

    Move from blank-template authoring to ambient-capture review-and-edit workflows.

  • Trustworthy AI

    Visible provenance, per-section verification, and gated sign-off so clinicians know what to trust.

  • SDOH at the point of care

    Surface social determinants where they can be addressed—not buried across screens.

  • Caregiver clarity

    Plain-language, status-first summaries that answer: Is my loved one okay? What changed? What do I need to do?

Projected impact: Reclaim time for patient interaction, reduce documentation-related burnout, increase confidence in AI-assisted tools, and improve care coordination for patients who rely on family support.

The Design Process

The work follows a six-phase healthcare UX process: Rethink → Redefine → Redesign → Revision → Refine → Launch.

It adapts classic Design Thinking for clinical constraints—understanding care context first, reframing problems through clinician- and patient-centered methods, and treating evaluation and iteration as distinct stages before launch. Methods draw on patterns used in health systems and health-tech product environments.

Pre-AI Documentation Burden Crisis

  • B1 Physicians spend approximately 2 hours on EHR work for every 1 hour of direct patient care. EHR-associated burden is a primary driver of physician burnout.
  • B2 For every patient encounter, clinicians spend an average of ~16 minutes on the EHR (chart review ~33%, documentation ~24%, ordering ~17%). Many spend 1–2+ hours after hours finishing documentation at home. After-hours work is a major burnout driver.

Ambient AI Adoption & Conditional Time Savings

  • T1 AI scribe adoption is associated with 13.4 fewer minutes of total EHR time and 16.0 fewer minutes of documentation time (difference-in-differences). Gains were greatest for primary care, APCs, female clinicians, and high users (≥50% of visits).
  • T2 Burnout rates decreased from 51.9% to 38.8% after 30 days of ambient AI scribe use (adjusted OR 0.26), with significant reductions in note-related cognitive task load and after-hours documentation time (−0.90 hours). However, clinicians must still review all AI outputs.
  • T3 Data shows a reduction in charting usability issues, but poor verification tools can offset these time savings.

AI Hallucinations, Omissions & Accuracy Issues

  • H1 Modern ambient AI scribes report hallucination rates of approximately 1–3% under narrow definitions, though rates rise (sometimes substantially) when broader clinical inconsistencies and omissions are included. Rates are generally higher in commercial summaries than carefully curated in-house records.
  • H2 Comparisons of AI-generated vs human or in-house notes show frequent omissions and excessive shortening in a substantial minority of cases (commonly 20–30% range across evaluations). Clinicians must treat AI content as drafts and review a large portion (often half or more) of generated notes.
  • H3 AI-fabricated errors can lead to poor monitoring and treatment errors if not caught during physician review. AI notes must be treated as drafts.
  • H4 In one evaluation, hallucinations were present in 31% of ambient AI notes (vs 20% of human “gold” notes). Other studies report that 20–50% of AI notes contain omissions or errors that could affect diagnostic or management decisions if not caught. AI notes must be treated as drafts requiring physician review.

Clinician Trust, Liability & Verification

  • V1 Misinterpreting commands or incorrect summaries can severely undermine clinician trust, which is as damaging to adoption as general inaccuracies.
  • V2 Clinician sentiment (paraphrased from qualitative studies): “If I don’t feel what’s in the note is what happened, then I won’t sign it. If I didn’t see it, I won’t sign it.” Trust requires that the note faithfully reflects the actual encounter.
  • V3 Clinicians remain deeply concerned about legal and clinical responsibility for signed AI outputs. Over-reliance is a significant liability risk.
  • V4 Large majorities of clinicians express concern about hallucinations and accuracy. Many report that ambient AI increases their perceived legal and clinical risk if verification tools are inadequate. Over-reliance remains a significant liability worry.

Workflow Impact

Editing & Review Load (Note Bloat)

  • E1 Note bloat from excessive verbatim capture increases clinician review time and downstream inefficiencies.
  • E2 Time saved on drafting is often partially offset by review, correction, and confirmation burden.
  • E3 AI scribe notes rated lower quality than human notes, especially on thoroughness and usefulness. AI = draft tool only.

Alert Fatigue & Visual Hierarchy Needs

  • A1 Clinicians receive dozens to hundreds of alerts daily; most low-value, leading to overrides and near-misses.
  • A2 AI features risk flooding interfaces unless strict visual hierarchy and prioritization are used.

Nursing Workflow Integration

  • N1 Many nurses batch-document vitals from memory/paper because desktop EHRs force them away from bedside—a patient safety risk.
  • N2 Mobile-first, low-tap bedside entry is critical. Tools are abandoned without immediate visible time savings.

User Needs & Design Solutions

Caregiver / Patient Needs

  • C1 AI-generated summaries often fail health literacy and emotional needs, causing caregiver distress and confusion.
  • C2 Jargon and lack of actionable synthesis are major barriers. Status-first + plain-language summaries are high-impact.

Design Recommendations & Validation of Lumen Chart Solutions

  • D1 Visual differentiation + provenance (AI vs verified text) is a recommended best practice. Inline color diff is standard in production.
  • D2 Amber diff highlighting + per-section “Accept” gates + two-gate signing directly address documented trust, verification, and liability gaps.
  • D3 Provenance/attribution and unmistakable visual distinction between AI-generated and verified content are critical for safe adoption.
  • D4 Design decisions (amber diff, transcript toggle, gated review, two-gate signing) respond directly to real-world frictions in 2023–2026 research.
  • D5 Benefits of ambient AI are real but fragile. They require hallucination detection, clear attribution, role-specific workflow, and curated alerts.

Participant 1 · 55-min interview

Dr. Laura Mitchell

Attending Physician, Internal Medicine · Conducted between morning clinic and afternoon rounds

Expand interview topics ↓ Collapse interview topics ↑

On daily EHR time and documentation burden

“Walk me through a typical clinic day. Where does your time actually go?”

“Honestly? I spend more time in the EHR than I do with patients. I track it sometimes — on a heavy day I’m in the chart for six, seven hours against eight hours of scheduled visits. That math doesn’t leave room for anything. I used to read journals. Now I’m just keeping up with inbox messages and prior auth requests.”
  • Spends an estimated 6–7 hrs/day in EHR on heavy clinic days — consistent with documentation-burden literature showing roughly 2 hours of EHR work per 1 hour of direct care (Sinsky et al., 2016; Arndt et al., 2017)
  • Reviews patient charts the night before (after-hours documentation) to prepare for next day — uncompensated cognitive labor identified in Sinsky 2016
  • Identifies inbox management and prior auth as the two biggest non-clinical time sinks
  • Has stopped reading journals due to documentation workload — signals risk of clinical knowledge degradation

Resolved by: Smart daily dashboard — surfaces inbox, alerts, and auth status in one view before the day starts

On pre-visit preparation

“How do you prepare before you walk into an exam room? What does that look like?”

“I’ll pull up the chart right before I knock on the door — sometimes in the hallway — and I’m trying to scan six months of notes in thirty seconds. I need to know why they’re here, what’s changed since last time, and what I need to cover. That’s the information I need. Instead I’m hunting through tabs.”
  • Pre-visit prep is compressed to under 60 seconds in the hallway — no structured pre-brief tool exists in current EHR
  • Key information needs: reason for visit, changes since last encounter, outstanding guideline gaps — exactly the AI snapshot agenda
  • Describes navigating multiple tabs to correlate labs, meds, and notes — matches critique of inability to correlate data across screens
  • Would strongly value a pre-populated, AI-curated summary she could trust if it told her what it was based on

Resolved by: AI patient snapshot — 6-month chart summary with provenance timestamp, trend labs, and suggested visit agenda

On AI documentation tools and trust

“Have you used or considered using ambient AI tools for documentation? What’s held you back or drawn you in?”

“I’ve tried one. It was impressive and terrifying at the same time. It got most things right but it hallucinated a medication that was never discussed. I had to read every word. The promise is real — I’d get back probably an hour a night — but I need to know exactly what it made up versus what it heard. Show me the difference.”
  • Has firsthand experience with ambient AI; primary barrier is inability to distinguish AI-generated text from verified content
  • Estimated time recovery of ~1 hr/night if trust issues were resolved — aligns with Twofold 2026 report of up to 75% documentation reduction
  • Specific ask: visual differentiation of AI-generated vs. chart-sourced text — the amber-highlight diff system in Screen 4
  • Wants transcript access as a ground-truth fallback — implemented as collapsible transcript panel
  • Will not sign a note she hasn’t reviewed section by section — confirms need for per-section accept flow and disabled sign button

Resolved by: SOAP editor with AI diff highlights, transcript panel, per-section accept, and pre-sign confirmation

On alerts and cognitive overload

“How do you experience clinical alerts in your current system? What do you do when they fire?”

“I click through them. I know I shouldn’t say that, but it’s true. When everything is urgent, nothing is urgent. I’ve been burned by alert fatigue — I once dismissed a drug interaction because I was clicking through thirty alerts in a row. The one that matters needs to look completely different from the ones that don’t.”
  • Admits to reflexive alert dismissal — a documented patient safety behavior pattern in high-alert-burden EHR environments
  • Describes a specific near-miss from alert fatigue — underscores the need for visual severity differentiation, not just volume reduction
  • Critical alerts need a distinct visual treatment (solid border, red) vs. informational ones — implemented in alert banner component
  • Wants alerts actionable from the dashboard, not requiring chart navigation to resolve

Resolved by: Alert panel with tiered severity system; critical flags surfaced at queue row level, not buried in chart

Participant 2 · 48-min interview

Kevin Hartley, RN

Charge Nurse, Medical-Surgical · Conducted at end of day shift before handoff

Expand interview topics ↓ Collapse interview topics ↑

On documentation timing and workarounds

“When in your shift do you typically document? Walk me through what that actually looks like.”

“Batch charting. I take vitals at 9, document them at 2. I write on my hand sometimes, or a paper towel. By the time I sit down I’m trying to remember what happened six hours ago with six patients. That’s not safe. I know it’s not safe. But I can’t leave a patient to go sit at a workstation every time I take a blood pressure.”
  • Batch-documents vitals hours after collection — a recognized safety risk, consistent with Penn Nursing finding that poor EHR usability contributes to worse patient outcomes
  • Uses paper (hand, paper towel) as a parallel documentation system — a workaround that signals complete failure of the digital tool to fit the workflow
  • Core constraint: no mobile-accessible, bedside-ready documentation interface in current system
  • Would document in real time if it required fewer than 3 taps at the point of care

Resolved by: Vitals block — single-screen, five-field entry with timestamp and capture method, optimized for speed

On patient deterioration and early warning

“Tell me about a time you caught something before it became a crisis. What tipped you off?”

“It was a subtle thing — the patient just looked different. But when I went to verify my gut, I had to open four different screens to correlate the BP trend, the latest labs, and the medication list. By the time I had everything in front of me, ten minutes had passed. Ten minutes matters. The data was all there — it just wasn’t together.”
  • Clinical intuition leads but verification requires navigating 4+ disconnected screens
  • Estimates 10-minute delay to assemble a complete clinical picture — time that is clinically significant in deterioration scenarios
  • Needs vitals trend, labs, and medication list co-located in one view — the patient snapshot accomplishes exactly this
  • Alert surfaced at queue level (not buried in chart) would reduce screens needed to identify who needs attention

Resolved by: Lab panel with sparkline trends co-located with vitals block and problem list in snapshot view

On shift handoff and knowledge transfer

“What’s the biggest risk during shift handoff? What information is most likely to fall through the gaps?”

“Anything that happened in the last two hours of my shift. I’m trying to give a verbal handoff while the incoming nurse is reading a note I wrote at 2pm that’s now six hours old. The note doesn’t reflect what’s actually happening right now. And if I ran out of time charting, the note might not exist at all.”
  • Verbal handoff is the primary safety mechanism when documentation is delayed — creates a single point of failure if interrupted
  • Handoff quality degrades proportionally to how far behind documentation has fallen — a systemic, not individual, failure
  • Ambient capture during patient interactions would produce a real-time record that doesn’t depend on charting time
  • Patient header persistently displaying current status, room, and visit type reduces re-briefing time during handoff review

Resolved by: Patient header always visible; ambient scribe produces contemporaneous record reducing handoff gap

On technology adoption and skepticism

“If a new clinical tool was introduced on your floor tomorrow, what would make you actually use it after the first week?”

“It would have to save me time within the first shift. Not eventually — within shift one. And it can’t require me to go to a computer to use it. I’m at the bedside. The tool has to come to me. The last three systems that got rolled out, I watched everyone go back to paper within two weeks. The tool has to fit the job. Not the other way.”
  • Adoption threshold: demonstrable time savings within a single shift — no patience for learning curves under operational pressure
  • Non-negotiable: mobile/tablet-first access at the point of care — desktop-tethered tools fail on a med-surg floor
  • Observed pattern of regression to paper after rollouts — confirms that poor fit with existing workflow is the primary adoption killer
  • Design implication: all components must meet 44px touch targets (WCAG 2.5.5); vitals and alert views must function on a tablet in portrait mode

Resolved by: Tablet patient queue with 3-tap vitals, queue-level deterioration flags, and bedside-first layout (Screen 2)

Participant 3 · 42-min interview

Diane Santos

Family Caregiver, daughter of patient Maria Santos · Conducted via phone during her lunch break

Expand interview topics ↓ Collapse interview topics ↑

On accessing information after appointments she missed

“When your mom has an appointment you weren’t able to attend, how do you find out what happened?”

“I call her. And she tells me what she remembers, which is maybe half of it. Then I try to log into the patient portal and I can never find what I’m looking for. There’s a ‘visit summary’ somewhere but it’s full of billing codes and abbreviations. It might as well be in another language. I just want to know: what did the doctor say, what changed, and what does mom need to do now.”
  • Primary information source is her mother’s verbal recall — inherently incomplete and inconsistent
  • Current patient portal visit summaries use clinical/billing language that is inaccessible to a non-clinical user
  • Core information need maps to three questions: what did the doctor say, what changed, what’s the next action
  • Would use the portal consistently if it answered those three questions in plain language without requiring navigation

Resolved by: Post-sign after-visit summary — plain-language, action-oriented, generated automatically (Screen 3)

On medication management and refills

“How do you currently manage keeping track of your mom’s medications — refills, supply, what’s changed?”

“I have a list in my phone that I update manually. But if the doctor changes a dose, half the time I don’t find out until mom runs out of the old pills and the new ones haven’t arrived yet. The Metformin situation right now — I don’t know if the insurance approved it or not. I’ve called the pharmacy twice. I just need someone to tell me: is it ready or not, and if not, why not, and who’s handling it.”
  • Maintains a manual medication list on her phone — a patient safety risk when clinical records and her list diverge
  • Dose changes don’t reach her reliably — the “New dose” badge on the caregiver view directly addresses this gap
  • Prior auth status is invisible to her; calls to two different organizations produce no definitive answer
  • Needs a single, authoritative status: resolved / pending / your action needed
  • Supply count and one-tap refill request would eliminate the pharmacy call loop

Resolved by: Caregiver medication view — supply tracker, “New dose” badge, auth status in plain language, one-tap refill request (Screen 6)

On understanding her role and access boundaries

“When you log into the patient portal as a caregiver, are you clear on what you’re allowed to do versus what only your mom can do?”

“Not really. I’ve been nervous to click certain things because I don’t know if it’ll send a message to the doctor that mom didn’t approve, or change something I shouldn’t change. I end up doing less than I probably could because I’m scared of overstepping. And I also don’t want my mom to feel like I’m taking over her care. That’s a real tension.”
  • Uncertainty about access boundaries leads to under-utilization of available features — she does less than the system permits
  • Worries about violating her mother’s autonomy — caregiver access design must feel collaborative, not surveillance-like
  • An explicit, always-visible permissions table removes the anxiety of guessing
  • “Access granted by Maria Santos on Apr 2, 2026” confirms consent is documented and visible

Resolved by: Role banner + permissions table in caregiver view — explicit can/can’t list, consent date, and “Manage access” link

On emotional experience and anxiety

“How do you feel when you check the portal or try to get an update on your mom’s health? What’s the emotional experience like?”

“Anxious, mostly. Because I usually can’t find what I need, and when I can’t find it I assume something’s wrong and nobody’s telling me. I know that’s probably not true, but that’s where my mind goes. If the system just told me ‘she’s okay, here’s what happened, here’s what to watch for’ — that would change everything. I don’t need to know every clinical detail. I need to know she’s okay.”
  • Information absence triggers anxiety — an empty or confusing interface is not neutral, it is actively distressing for a caregiver
  • Core emotional need: reassurance, not information density — designed around calm, plain-language status
  • Explicitly does not want clinical detail — confirms withholding drug interaction data from caregiver views
  • The “all clear” and “Dr. Mitchell’s office is working on it” micro-copy patterns directly serve this emotional need
  • Every empty state, status message, and error in the caregiver view must be written from a reassurance-first perspective

Resolved by: Status-first caregiver portal home with persistent “Maria is doing well” banner (Screen 3)

Cross-cutting themes — from the interviews

Qualitative patterns that showed up across Laura, Kevin, and Diane—what participants said and where their workflows broke down.

Theme 1

Documentation time is the primary system failure

All three participants describe documentation consuming time that should belong to patients, recovery, or care coordination. Laura documents at night; Kevin batches at shift end; Diane wastes time on phone calls a status field could answer in seconds.

B1–B2

Theme 2

Trust in AI requires transparency, not just accuracy

Laura will use ambient AI but needs to see what it generated vs. what came from the chart. Accuracy alone is insufficient — the system must make its own uncertainty visible.

H1–H4 · V1–V4 · D1–D3

Theme 3

Alert fatigue is a hierarchy problem, not a volume problem

Laura dismisses alerts reflexively because they all look the same. Kevin misses flags because they require chart navigation. The fix is tiered visual severity and queue-level surfacing.

A1 · A2

Theme 4

The tool must meet users where they are

Kevin is at the bedside. Diane is on her phone between meetings. Laura is in a hallway. Every screen must function under interruption, on a touch device, in under 60 seconds of available attention.

N1 · N2 · WCAG 2.5.5

Theme 5

For caregivers, ambiguity is an emotional harm

Diane’s anxiety is a predictable response to a system that withholds clarity. Plain language, permission transparency, and status reassurance are safety features — not nice-to-haves.

Early Problem Framing

After synthesizing the evidence wall and interview insights, several problem spaces emerged:

  • ◆How might we reduce the 6–7 hours many primary care clinicians spend in the EHR each day?
  • ◆How might we make AI-generated documentation trustworthy enough that clinicians will actually sign it?
  • ◆How might we surface critical SDOH factors at the point of care instead of burying them?
  • ◆How might we give family caregivers plain-language answers to their three core questions: Is my loved one okay? What changed? What do I need to do?

These early questions informed the final How Might We statement refined in the Redefine phase.

From Evidence to Product

Where the published literature shaped specific Lumen Chart decisions. Unlike the interview themes above, these are literature-led bridges from evidence to interface—not a retelling of participant stories.

Literature

Ambient AI savings are real—and conditional

Multisite data associates AI scribes with 13.4 fewer minutes of total EHR time and 16.0 fewer minutes of documentation (difference-in-differences), plus burnout drops after 30 days of use. Gains are greatest for primary care and high users, and erode when verification is poor.

T1–T3 · B1–B2

In Lumen Chart

Compress prep; protect the time saved

Unified pre-visit snapshot and ambient SOAP drafts reduce hunting across tabs. Review is structured so the minutes reclaimed in drafting are not spent again in unstructured cleanup.

Literature

AI notes are drafts with measurable error risk

Hallucination rates of ~1–3% under narrow definitions rise when omissions count; one evaluation found hallucinations in 31% of ambient notes vs 20% of human gold notes. Clinicians will not sign what they cannot attribute or verify.

H1–H4 · V1–V4 · E1–E3

In Lumen Chart

Provenance, amber diff, two-gate sign

AI vs chart text is visually distinct; per-section Accept gates and a two-gate signing flow make review mandatory and liability-aware before the note is finalized.

Literature

Alert value depends on visual priority

High daily alert volume drives overrides and near-misses. Adding AI without hierarchy multiplies noise. Severity, placement, and weight matter more than raw count reduction.

A1–A2 · D5

In Lumen Chart

Critical alerts at queue level

Rose-tier critical signals surface on the patient queue and dashboard; routine items stay demoted so clinicians can trust what demands attention.

Literature

One EHR serves three incompatible contexts

Nursing documentation burden research shows bedside tools fail when they force desktop workflows. Caregiver-facing AI often fails literacy and emotional needs. Role-specific surfaces are a safety requirement, not a nice-to-have.

N1–N2 · C1–C2 · D5

In Lumen Chart

Physician, nurse, and caregiver views

Laura’s ambient editor, Kevin’s tablet vitals queue, and Diane’s plain-language meds and after-visit summary are designed as parallel products sharing one patient record—not one UI stretched to fit everyone.

02 Redefine — User Flows & Information Architecture

Final “How might we”

How might we redesign EHR clinical data review and documentation workflows so that primary care clinicians can efficiently manage information and charting tasks, reclaim more time for patients, and create trustworthy, plain-language outputs that help family caregivers stay informed — while reducing burnout risk?

Why this framing

  • ◆It centers both efficiency and the human outcome (reclaiming time for patients).
  • ◆It explicitly includes the trust problem created by AI—a major finding from research.
  • ◆It keeps the door open for caregiver needs without making the statement too broad.
  • ◆It is actionable for interface design while staying grounded in the real constraints of primary care.

This reframed question became the north star for all subsequent design decisions.

Information Architecture — Site Map

EHR Provider Interface Conceptual Sitemap

Designed a five-column vertical information architecture for an EHR provider interface that reduces navigation depth to a maximum of two clicks from the Home dashboard while better aligning with real clinical workflows.

The structure organizes the experience around five primary clinical activities: Today’s Schedule, Patient Chart, Documentation, Team & Handoff, and Settings & Profile. Each section uses consistent visual hierarchy and horizontally aligned rows to minimize cognitive load and visual scanning time.

Key design decisions were informed by:

  • ◆Research on EHR usability challenges, including excessive navigation depth, workflow mismatch, and high cognitive load (e.g., studies on “death by a thousand clicks” and documentation burden).
  • ◆Human factors and NIST-aligned principles emphasizing shallow information architecture, task-oriented navigation, consistency, and support for team-based care.
  • ◆Evidence that dedicated support for handoffs and team coordination improves safety and reduces clinician friction.

By treating Documentation and Team & Handoff as first-class top-level sections (rather than burying them), the architecture directly addresses two of the most significant drivers of clinician burnout and workflow disruption in current EHR systems.
