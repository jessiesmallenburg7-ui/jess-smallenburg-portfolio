TraumaTrack A Trauma-Informed Digital Platform for High-Fidelity TF-CBT Delivery, Real-Time Fidelity Monitoring, and Longitudinal Outcome Evaluation in Community Clinics

From Evaluation Plan to User-Centered Design: Supporting high-fidelity Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT) delivery and longitudinal outcome measurement in community clinics

In Progress

Project Overview

TraumaTrack began as an evaluation plan developed for a Measurement and Evaluation course in Wayne State University’s M.S. Community Health Education program. The plan called for a rigorous comparison of Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT) versus Therapy As Usual (TAU) across 300 youth ages 10–18 with post-traumatic stress symptoms (PTSS) in community outpatient clinics, with five data collection timepoints and ongoing fidelity monitoring using the TF-CBT PRACTICE Fidelity Checklist.

This case study shows how that evaluation framework was translated into user-centered digital design—balancing HIPAA-compliant clinical workflows with real-time fidelity monitoring and longitudinal outcome evaluation—to address the practical challenges of delivering and measuring an evidence-based trauma treatment in real-world community clinics.

Evaluation Questions This Project Addresses

TraumaTrack was designed to directly support the four core evaluation questions from the TF-CBT Evaluation Plan:

  1. 1. Process Evaluation (Fidelity) — To what extent was the TF-CBT intervention implemented with fidelity to the core PRACTICE components in community clinics?
  2. 2. Short-term Impact — Are there measurable reductions in internalizing symptoms (worries, fears, and emotion regulation difficulties) as a result of the intervention? (measured via SDQ Emotional Symptoms subscale)
  3. 3. Intermediate-term Impact — Are there measurable improvements in perceived social support (measured via the Duke-UNC Functional Social Support Questionnaire – FSSQ) and coping compared to Therapy as Usual (TAU)?
  4. 4. Long-term Outcome — Are there broad improvements in mental health outcomes and quality of life in this population 12–18 months post-intervention compared to TAU?

Evaluation Context

The study uses a randomized design in which 150 youth receive TF-CBT and 150 receive TAU. Primary measures include the Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire (SDQ) Emotional Symptoms subscale, the Duke-UNC Functional Social Support Questionnaire (FSSQ; Broadhead et al., 1988) as used in Birkeland et al. (2020), and eight custom items. Data are collected from youth and caregivers at five timepoints: baseline, mid-intervention (after session 6), post-intervention (after 12–15 sessions), 12-month follow-up, and 18-month follow-up.

Process evaluation uses the TF-CBT Fidelity Checklist to track adherence to PRACTICE components. Planned analyses include repeated-measures ANOVA, ANCOVA, longitudinal mixed-effects models, descriptive fidelity statistics, and qualitative theme analysis of open-ended responses.

Clinical Framework — TF-CBT PRACTICE

TF-CBT is delivered through the PRACTICE model:

  • ◆Psychoeducation & parenting skills
  • ◆Relaxation
  • ◆Affective modulation
  • ◆Cognitive coping
  • ◆Trauma narrative
  • ◆In vivo mastery
  • ◆Conjoint child–parent sessions
  • ◆Enhancing safety & future development

Goals & Expected Outcomes

The evaluation aims for high-fidelity TF-CBT delivery in real-world community settings; short-term reductions in internalizing symptoms (worries, fears, and emotion regulation); intermediate improvements in perceived social support (FSSQ) and coping relative to TAU; and long-term gains in overall mental health outcomes and quality of life. Projected results include statistically significant symptom reduction plus sustained improvements of at least 45% in internalizing symptoms and 40% in social support at 5–10 years post-intervention.

TraumaTrack translates that research infrastructure into a HIPAA-compliant digital platform that embeds automated fidelity monitoring, seamless multi-timepoint data collection (including FSSQ), and clinician-friendly outcome visualization into everyday TF-CBT workflows—especially in resource-limited community clinics where paper checklists and fragmented tools make fidelity and longitudinal outcomes hard to sustain.

Personas

Why Personas?

I developed these three research-grounded personas (Nia the youth, Marcus the clinician, and Denise — Nia’s mother and caregiver) to bridge the TF-CBT Evaluation Plan’s stratified purposive sample, five timepoint data collection, and key instruments — including the Duke-UNC Functional Social Support Questionnaire (FSSQ) — into concrete, empathetic design decisions.

Rather than treating demographics and clinical variables as abstract data points, personas humanized the distinct realities of trauma-exposed youth, overburdened community clinicians, and time-constrained caregivers in resource-limited Southeastern Michigan clinics. This was critical for embedding trauma-informed principles (safety, choice, trustworthiness, collaboration, empowerment) from the start, ensuring TraumaTrack would improve fidelity monitoring, reduce administrative burden, support FSSQ-based social support tracking, and minimize dropout risk without adding harm. Personas kept the entire design process tightly aligned with the four evaluation questions and real-world implementation barriers.

Demographics

Black, she/her, lives in a mid-sized Southeastern Michigan community with her mother, Denise, and younger brother. Denise works two part-time retail jobs; household near the poverty line. Attends public middle school; unstable home Wi-Fi, reliable phone data.

Trauma & Clinical Profile

Exposure to domestic violence and psychological abuse at home. Screens in the abnormal band (7–10) on the SDQ Emotional Symptoms subscale. No prior therapy. Low baseline perceived social support, consistent with the low-support pattern seen in youth entering trauma therapy with high symptom levels.

Goals & Motivations

  • Stop feeling on-edge at home
  • Keep friendships that don’t require “the whole story”
  • Have real control over what she shares, and when

Frustrations & Pain Points

  • Paper mood forms feel like homework and go missing
  • Repeating her story to new adults
  • Fear Denise will see private answers

Tech Behaviors & Access

Personal smartphone is her primary device. Moderate-to-high digital literacy; prefers texting and apps over calls; wary of anything that feels like it’s “reporting” on her.

How TraumaTrack Helps

Youth check-ins replace paper SDQ forms. Granular sharing controls show exactly what Denise and her therapist can see. Progress view is tied to her own goals, not clinic metrics.

Design Implications

  • 01 Default all narrative/journal entries to private; require an explicit, reversible opt-in to share with Denise.
  • 02 Use warm, non-clinical microcopy — “check in,” not “assessment.”
  • 03 Never auto-notify Denise of a symptom score without Nia’s awareness.
  • 04 Offer a low-stakes preview of any check-in before it “counts,” to reduce first-use anxiety.

Safety Choice Trustworthiness Collaboration Empowerment

Demographics

Licensed clinical social worker at a community outpatient mental health clinic. Caseload of 30+, roughly a third trauma-focused. Limited admin support; uses a personal laptop due to outdated clinic hardware.

Clinical Context

Trained in the TF-CBT PRACTICE model — Psychoeducation and parenting skills, Relaxation, Affective modulation, Cognitive coping, Trauma narrative, In vivo mastery, Conjoint sessions, and Enhancing safety — delivered across roughly 12–15 sessions in three near-equal phases.

Goals & Motivations

  • Deliver every PRACTICE component with fidelity
  • Keep families engaged through the full course of treatment
  • Document efficiently enough to still have time with clients

Frustrations & Pain Points

  • Paper fidelity checklists completed after the fact lose accuracy
  • Caregiver no-shows disrupt the parallel-session structure
  • No easy view of a client’s trend across five research timepoints
  • Full PRACTICE-model fidelity is genuinely hard under high caseloads and limited resources, especially when caregivers face their own barriers like poverty, mental illness, or substance use
  • Real-world community outcomes run more modest than tightly controlled efficacy trials — hard to explain to families expecting fast results

Tech Behaviors & Access

Comfortable with EHR-style software; moderate patience for clunky systems. Values anything that reduces documentation time without adding clicks.

How TraumaTrack Helps

In-session fidelity checklist mirrors the PRACTICE sequence in real time. Dashboard flags missed caregiver-participation thresholds and auto-populates the longitudinal symptom and support data across all five assessment points.

Design Implications

  • 01 Structure the fidelity checklist by PRACTICE phase, markable during the session rather than reconstructed afterward.
  • 02 Surface caregiver-participation status prominently — parallel caregiver sessions are integral, not optional.
  • 03 Provide one longitudinal symptom/support trendline per client spanning all five timepoints.
  • 04 Minimize documentation friction — every extra click competes directly with session time.
  • 05 Frame outcome dashboards against real-world community benchmarks, not idealized efficacy-trial curves, so clinicians and families aren’t measured against an unrealistic bar.

Demographics

Black, she/her, single mother of Nia (14) and a younger son. Works two part-time retail jobs with irregular hours; household near the poverty line in a mid-sized Southeastern Michigan community. Limited paid time off; relies partly on public transit and phone data when home Wi-Fi is unstable.

Role in Treatment

Non-offending parent and Nia’s primary caregiver in TF-CBT. Expected to complete a minimum of three parallel caregiver sessions and provide informant reports at each research timepoint. Her own reactions to hearing her daughter’s trauma narrative are an expected, normal part of the parallel-session process.

Goals & Motivations

  • Help Nia heal without being made to feel at fault
  • Keep appointments despite an unpredictable retail schedule
  • Understand what happens in sessions she can’t attend

Frustrations & Pain Points

  • Session times conflict with dual retail shifts
  • Paper handouts get lost between clinic and home
  • Real logistical and emotional barriers to staying consistently involved

Tech Behaviors & Access

Older-model smartphone, moderate digital literacy. Checks her phone mainly during breaks between shifts; prefers short push notifications over emails she won’t open.

How TraumaTrack Helps

Mobile-first portal for completing informant reports about Nia asynchronously. Session-summary micro-updates after visits she misses. Push reminders timed around her retail breaks, not a fixed 9-to-5 assumption.

Design Implications

  • 01 Keep informant report forms short and mobile-first — long forms go uncompleted under dual-job schedule constraints.
  • 02 Never frame missed sessions punitively in-app; offer easy rescheduling instead.
  • 03 Provide brief, plain-language session recaps so Denise can stay informed when she can’t attend.
  • 04 Time reminders flexibly around retail hours, not a standard workday assumption.

Why Journey Maps?

I developed Current-State and Ideal-State journey maps for Nia (youth) and Marcus (clinician) to visualize the end-to-end experience across the five evaluation timepoints in real-world TF-CBT delivery.

By mapping their parallel journeys against the four evaluation questions — with emphasis on PRACTICE fidelity and longitudinal SDQ/FSSQ tracking — the maps surfaced key barriers including documentation burden, privacy risks, high dropout potential, and fragmented data processes.

This analysis translated the evaluation plan and personas into clear design priorities, directly shaping TraumaTrack’s core features (real-time fidelity checklist, automated surveys, privacy controls, and longitudinal dashboards) to reduce administrative load, uphold trauma-informed principles, and improve implementation fidelity and youth outcomes.
