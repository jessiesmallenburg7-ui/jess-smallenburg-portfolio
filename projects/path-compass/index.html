Path Compass

Your guide on the path to healing.

From evaluation plan to user-centered design — supporting high-fidelity trauma-focused care through guided progress in community mental health.

Project Overview

“How might we”

How might we support high-fidelity TF-CBT delivery in community clinics while giving youth greater agency over their information and reducing documentation burden for clinicians and caregivers?

Design focus

A HIPAA-aware clinical system that keeps PRACTICE fidelity, longitudinal outcomes, and trauma-informed privacy in one workflow.

  • Fidelity Session-level PRACTICE checklist
  • Outcomes SDQ & FSSQ across five timepoints
  • Privacy Trauma-informed consent & control

Path Compass is a speculative UX project grounded in a master’s-level program evaluation for Wayne State University’s M.S. in Community Health Education (Measurement and Evaluation).

The evaluation plan compared Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT) with Therapy As Usual (TAU) for 300 youth ages 10–18 with post-traumatic stress symptoms in community outpatient clinics.

It required five data collection timepoints and ongoing fidelity monitoring with the TF-CBT PRACTICE Fidelity Checklist (Deblinger et al., 2014).

This case study translates that framework into user-centered design: how systems-level tooling could support high-fidelity TF-CBT delivery and outcome measurement in real-world clinics.

Design Process

Path Compass follows a five-phase process that moves from the evaluation plan into trauma-informed product design—with research synthesis, competitive pattern analysis, and journey mapping as the primary discovery methods.

  1. 01 Foundation & Research

    Grounded the work in the existing TF-CBT Evaluation Plan: study design, SDQ/FSSQ instruments, five timepoints, and PRACTICE fidelity requirements. Reviewed clinical constraints in community outpatient settings and trauma-informed digital design literature. Defined non-negotiables early—HIPAA-aware workflows, youth agency over disclosure, and documentation that can keep pace with session care.

  2. 02 Synthesis & Insight Generation

    Translated the evaluation plan into three research-grounded personas—youth, clinician, and caregiver—representing the primary roles across the product. Built Current State and Ideal State journey maps across evaluation phases to surface friction: documentation burden, privacy risk, dropout pressure, and fragmented outcome tracking. Those insights became design priorities: live PRACTICE fidelity, automated multi-timepoint surveys, and trauma-informed privacy controls.

  3. 03 Exploration & Benchmarking

    Benchmarked adjacent tools against the jobs Path Compass must cover: real-time PRACTICE fidelity, five-timepoint SDQ/FSSQ collection for youth and caregivers, and multi-party privacy. No single product owns that stack. Pattern comparisons clarified the product wedge versus paper checklists, TF-CBT training platforms, in-session toolkits, and measurement-based care systems.

  4. 04 Design & Prototyping

    Moving from insights into interface: information architecture and task flows that mirror PRACTICE sequencing and evaluation timepoints. Wireframes and high-fidelity mockups for the youth app, clinician dashboard, and caregiver portal will culminate in an interactive prototype of the end-to-end experience.

  5. 05 Evaluation & Validation

    Validation focuses on heuristic evaluation against usability heuristics and trauma-informed digital principles, complemented by expert review with clinical and UX stakeholders. Findings will refine accessibility, cognitive load, and trust cues before any future live evaluation.

Path Compass is a conceptual / speculative UX case study grounded in an existing mixed-methods TF-CBT Evaluation Plan. Because of project scope, validation centers on heuristic evaluation and expert review rather than primary generative or evaluative user research with clinic participants.

01 Foundation & Research

Before designing screens, the evaluation plan and clinical model set the problem boundary: what fidelity, measures, and TF-CBT structure the product must support.

Evaluation Context

A randomized design assigns 150 youth to TF-CBT and 150 to TAU.

  • Primary measures

    SDQ Emotional Symptoms subscale (worries, fears, emotion regulation) and the Duke-UNC FSSQ (Broadhead et al., 1988; as used in Birkeland et al., 2020), plus eight custom items.

  • Five timepoints

    SDQ and FSSQ from youth and caregivers at baseline, mid-intervention (session 6), post-intervention (sessions 12–15), 12-month follow-up, and 18-month follow-up.

  • Process & analysis

    TF-CBT Fidelity Checklist for PRACTICE adherence. Planned analyses: repeated-measures ANOVA, ANCOVA, mixed-effects models, descriptive fidelity stats, and qualitative theme analysis.

Clinical Framework — TF-CBT PRACTICE

TF-CBT is delivered through the PRACTICE model:

  • Psychoeducation & parenting skills Normalize trauma reactions and equip caregivers with supportive strategies.
  • Relaxation Build body-based calming skills youth can use when distress rises.
  • Affective modulation Identify, name, and regulate difficult feelings in safer ways.
  • Cognitive coping Notice unhelpful thoughts and practice more balanced alternatives.
  • Trauma narrative Process the trauma story gradually to reduce avoidance and distress.
  • In vivo mastery Face safe reminders of trauma in real life to restore a sense of control.
  • Conjoint child–parent sessions Share progress together and strengthen trust, communication, and support.
  • Enhancing safety & future development Plan for ongoing safety and healthy next steps after treatment ends.

Evaluation Questions

Path Compass was designed to directly support the four core evaluation questions from the TF-CBT Evaluation Plan:

  1. 1
    Process Evaluation (Fidelity)

    To what extent was the TF-CBT intervention implemented with fidelity to the core PRACTICE components in community clinics?

  2. 2
    Short-term Impact

    Are there measurable reductions in internalizing symptoms (worries, fears, and emotion regulation difficulties) as a result of the intervention? (measured via SDQ Emotional Symptoms subscale)

  3. 3
    Intermediate-term Impact

    Are there measurable improvements in perceived social support (measured via the Duke-UNC Functional Social Support Questionnaire – FSSQ) and coping compared to Therapy as Usual (TAU)?

  4. 4
    Long-term Outcome

    Are there broad improvements in mental health outcomes and quality of life in this population 12–18 months post-intervention compared to TAU?

Goals & Expected Outcomes

The evaluation targets measurable gains across fidelity and outcomes in community settings.

  • High-fidelity delivery

    Sustain PRACTICE adherence in real-world community clinics—not just research sites.

  • Symptom & support gains

    Short-term reductions in internalizing symptoms; intermediate FSSQ and coping gains vs. TAU; longer-term mental health and quality-of-life improvements.

  • Projected durability

    Statistically significant symptom reduction, with sustained improvements of at least 45% in internalizing symptoms and 40% in social support at 5–10 years post-intervention.

  • Platform translation

    Embed fidelity monitoring, multi-timepoint data collection, and outcome visualization into everyday TF-CBT workflows—especially where paper checklists and fragmented tools fail.

02 Synthesis & Insight Generation

Personas and journey maps convert evaluation requirements into lived experience—where fidelity, privacy, and longitudinal data collection collide in real clinic life.

Personas

Why Personas?

Three research-grounded personas—Nia (youth), Marcus (clinician), and Denise (caregiver)—translate the evaluation plan’s sample, instruments, and five timepoints into concrete design decisions.

They keep trauma-informed principles (safety, choice, trustworthiness, collaboration, empowerment) front and center, so fidelity monitoring and outcome tracking reduce burden without adding harm.

Nia, 14

Youth Participant · Mobile App

Age 14 Youth · Mobile App Domestic Violence Exposure SDQ: Abnormal Band No Prior Therapy Southeastern Michigan
“I don’t want another form. I want to know that what I say here actually stays mine unless I say otherwise.”

Trauma-informed care needs emphasized

Safety Choice Trustworthiness Collaboration Empowerment

Demographics

She/her, lives in a mid-sized Southeastern Michigan community with her mother, Denise, and younger brother. Denise works two part-time retail jobs; household near the poverty line. Attends public middle school; unstable home Wi-Fi, reliable phone data.

Trauma & Clinical Profile

Exposure to domestic violence and psychological abuse at home. Screens in the abnormal band (7–10) on the SDQ Emotional Symptoms subscale. No prior therapy. Low baseline perceived social support, consistent with the low-support pattern seen in youth entering trauma therapy with high symptom levels.

Goals & Motivations

  • Stop feeling on-edge at home
  • Keep friendships that don’t require “the whole story”
  • Have real control over what she shares, and when

Frustrations & Pain Points

  • Paper mood forms feel like homework and go missing
  • Repeating her story to new adults
  • Fear Denise will see private answers

Tech Behaviors & Access

Personal smartphone is her primary device. Moderate-to-high digital literacy; prefers texting and apps over calls; wary of anything that feels like it’s “reporting” on her.

How Path Compass Helps

Youth check-ins replace paper SDQ forms. Granular sharing controls show exactly what Denise and her therapist can see. Progress view is tied to her own goals, not clinic metrics.

Design Implications

  • 01 Default all narrative/journal entries to private; require an explicit, reversible opt-in to share with Denise.
  • 02 Use warm, non-clinical microcopy — “check in,” not “assessment.”
  • 03 Never auto-notify Denise of a symptom score without Nia’s awareness.
  • 04 Offer a low-stakes preview of any check-in before it “counts,” to reduce first-use anxiety.

Marcus, 38

Clinician · Web Dashboard & Fidelity Tools

Age 38 LCSW · Dashboard 30+ Caseload PRACTICE-Trained Community Outpatient Clinic Limited Admin Support
“If I have to choose between finishing a fidelity form and actually being present with a kid mid-narrative, I choose the kid every time.”

Trauma-informed care needs emphasized

Safety Choice Trustworthiness Collaboration Empowerment

Demographics

Licensed clinical social worker at a community outpatient mental health clinic. Caseload of 30+, roughly a third trauma-focused. Limited admin support; uses a personal laptop due to outdated clinic hardware.

Clinical Context

Trained in the TF-CBT PRACTICE model — Psychoeducation and parenting skills, Relaxation, Affective modulation, Cognitive coping, Trauma narrative, In vivo mastery, Conjoint sessions, and Enhancing safety — delivered across roughly 12–15 sessions in three near-equal phases.

Goals & Motivations

  • Deliver every PRACTICE component with fidelity
  • Keep families engaged through the full course of treatment
  • Document efficiently enough to still have time with clients

Frustrations & Pain Points

  • Paper fidelity checklists completed after the fact lose accuracy
  • Caregiver no-shows disrupt the parallel-session structure
  • No easy view of a client’s trend across five research timepoints
  • Full PRACTICE-model fidelity is genuinely hard under high caseloads and limited resources, especially when caregivers face their own barriers like poverty, mental illness, or substance use
  • Real-world community outcomes run more modest than tightly controlled efficacy trials — hard to explain to families expecting fast results

Tech Behaviors & Access

Comfortable with EHR-style software; moderate patience for clunky systems. Values anything that reduces documentation time without adding clicks.

How Path Compass Helps

In-session fidelity checklist mirrors the PRACTICE sequence in real time. Dashboard flags missed caregiver-participation thresholds and auto-populates the longitudinal symptom and support data across all five assessment points.

Design Implications

  • 01 Structure the fidelity checklist by PRACTICE phase, markable during the session rather than reconstructed afterward.
  • 02 Surface caregiver-participation status prominently — parallel caregiver sessions are integral, not optional.
  • 03 Provide one longitudinal symptom/support trendline per client spanning all five timepoints.
  • 04 Minimize documentation friction — every extra click competes directly with session time.
  • 05 Frame outcome dashboards against real-world community benchmarks, not idealized efficacy-trial curves, so clinicians and families aren’t measured against an unrealistic bar.

Demographics

She/her, single mother of Nia (14) and a younger son. Works two part-time retail jobs with irregular hours; household near the poverty line in a mid-sized Southeastern Michigan community. Limited paid time off; relies partly on public transit and phone data when home Wi-Fi is unstable.

Role in Treatment

Non-offending parent and Nia’s primary caregiver in TF-CBT. Expected to complete a minimum of three parallel caregiver sessions and provide informant reports at each research timepoint. Her own reactions to hearing her daughter’s trauma narrative are an expected, normal part of the parallel-session process.

Goals & Motivations

  • Help Nia heal without being made to feel at fault
  • Keep appointments despite an unpredictable retail schedule
  • Understand what happens in sessions she can’t attend

Frustrations & Pain Points

  • Session times conflict with dual retail shifts
  • Paper handouts get lost between clinic and home
  • Real logistical and emotional barriers to staying consistently involved

Tech Behaviors & Access

Older-model smartphone, moderate digital literacy. Checks her phone mainly during breaks between shifts; prefers short push notifications over emails she won’t open.

How Path Compass Helps

Mobile-first portal for completing informant reports about Nia asynchronously. Session-summary micro-updates after visits she misses. Push reminders timed around her retail breaks, not a fixed 9-to-5 assumption.

Design Implications

  • 01 Keep informant report forms short and mobile-first — long forms go uncompleted under dual-job schedule constraints.
  • 02 Never frame missed sessions punitively in-app; offer easy rescheduling instead.
  • 03 Provide brief, plain-language session recaps so Denise can stay informed when she can’t attend.
  • 04 Time reminders flexibly around retail hours, not a standard workday assumption.

03 Exploration & Benchmarking

Competitive analysis pressure-tests the journey findings against tools clinics already touch—and names the incomplete clusters Path Compass is designed to fill.

Competitive Benchmarking

After mapping Nia and Marcus’s journeys, I benchmarked adjacent tools against the jobs Path Compass must cover: real-time PRACTICE fidelity, five-timepoint SDQ and FSSQ collection from youth and caregivers, trauma-informed privacy, and community-clinic documentation load. No single product owns that stack—so the comparison clarifies Path Compass’s wedge rather than chasing EHR feature parity.

Why Benchmark?

Competitive review pressure-tested the journey findings against what clinics already use for TF-CBT training, in-session engagement, measurement-based care (MBC), and AI quality improvement. The goal was to name incomplete clusters in the landscape and protect design priorities that would otherwise get diluted by “just use our existing EHR” or “add another PROM tool.”

Shortlist

Six competitors (plus paper status quo) were included if they were TF-CBT/PRACTICE-aligned, common in community behavioral-health quality improvement (QI), or a realistic artifact Marcus would already touch. Full-practice EHRs with optional MBC widgets were treated as adjacent noise, not primary competitors.

Four Incomplete Clusters

These four clusters represent the current market gaps that Path Compass is designed to address:

  1. 1
    Train & Certify

    TF-CBTWeb and similar platforms effectively teach the PRACTICE model, but they do not support real-world delivery workflows, fidelity logging during sessions, or longitudinal evaluation in community settings.

  2. 2
    Engage in Session

    Tools like SPARK help structure in-session activities and support fidelity in the moment, but they do not provide multi-timepoint outcome tracking or serve as a shared workspace across youth, caregivers, and clinicians.

  3. 3
    Measure Broadly

    Greenspace and EHR measurement-based care add-ons excel at collecting and trending patient-reported outcomes, but they are not built around TF-CBT fidelity monitoring or trauma-informed privacy and narrative-sharing controls.

  4. 4
    Score & Supervise with AI

    Platforms like Lyssn use AI to score session quality and support supervision at scale. However, they function primarily as organizational quality tools rather than collaborative workspaces for youth, caregivers, and clinicians.

Path Compass’s wedge

A clinic workspace that joins PRACTICE fidelity, five-timepoint SDQ/FSSQ collection, and trauma-informed multi-party privacy in one continuum—the gap the journeys hit when paper and fragmented tools collide.
