Usability Analysis

Improving Findability of Eligibility & Enrollment Information on a Public Health Benefits Portal

Project Overview

A major public health benefits portal was receiving high volumes of visitors seeking coverage and eligibility information for themselves or family members. Many struggled to locate clear pathways to enrollment details or state-specific resources. The goal was to identify the primary barriers preventing high-intent users from finding the information they needed and to recommend targeted improvements that would reduce friction and improve access.

The Challenge

  • A significant percentage of visitors arrived with the explicit goal of finding coverage or eligibility information.
  • Key calls-to-action were underperforming relative to user intent.
  • Navigation and content discoverability issues led to abandonment or over-reliance on search.
  • Content readability appeared mismatched with the literacy levels and needs of the target audience (often including individuals navigating government systems under time pressure or with varying health literacy).

These barriers mattered because they directly affected timely access to essential health coverage for vulnerable populations.

My Role

As a Usability Analyst, I conducted behavioral UX research and analysis for a client’s public health benefits platform. I led mixed-methods analysis (session replays, heatmaps, and survey synthesis) to identify key usability barriers for users seeking coverage and eligibility information. I delivered prioritized insights and actionable recommendations directly to the client.

Research Approach & Methods

I employed a mixed-methods approach centered on real user behavior:

  • 01

    Session replay & heatmap analysis

    Reviewed how visitors actually interacted with critical pages (homepage, navigation, and resource/search results pages). This revealed scrolling patterns, hesitation, click behaviors, and navigation struggles in real time.

  • 02

    Survey data synthesis

    Analyzed responses from recent site visitors, focusing on those seeking coverage information. Prioritized negative satisfaction signals, primary visit reasons, and open-ended comments about findability and comprehension.

  • 03

    Prioritization framework

    Evaluated findings based on negative feedback volume, coverage-seeking intent, and site information quality metrics.

Why these methods?

Session replays and heatmaps show what users actually do (vs. what they report), which is particularly valuable for high-stakes public services where users face cognitive load, stress, or time constraints. Pairing this with survey comments provided rich context on the why.

Key Insights

Four core barriers emerged from the analysis:

  • Insight 01

    Primary Call-to-Action Lacked Prominence

    The main pathway for coverage-seeking visitors was not visually distinct. It blended into the page design, lacked standard button styling or hover feedback, and was sometimes positioned below the fold. Many users who wanted enrollment information scrolled past it or failed to recognize it as interactive. Feedback frequently referenced the need for clearer enrollment directions.

  • Insight 02

    Complex Megamenu Navigation

    Visitors spent significant time hovering over global navigation without selecting options, often switching to search instead. This indicated the menu was overly broad or deep for users needing quick access to eligibility or enrollment resources.

  • Insight 03

    Buried Pagination on Resource Pages

    On documentation and resource listing pages, few visitors scrolled far enough to reach pagination controls, leaving additional valuable content undiscovered.

  • Insight 04

    Readability & Health Literacy Gap

    Open-ended comments repeatedly noted that content was difficult to understand and overly complex. A formal readability assessment of key eligibility pages confirmed the gap—detail and representative quotes follow below.

Insight 04 in depth

Content Readability Barriers

During a usability evaluation of a major public benefits website, open-ended survey responses repeatedly highlighted difficulty understanding the information. Many participants described the language as overly complex and not written for a general audience.

Representative comments

  • “This is not presented in layman’s terms. You need a PhD to make out all the nuances in the words.”

  • “There were no clear answers or direction to find a simple answer to what the eligibility requirements are.”

  • “Need to make it easier to understand what is related to your question. Just use simple terms that most of us can understand.”

  • “The website needs to be simple and easy to understand and navigate.”

  • “Put things into layman’s terms. Just keep it simple.”

  • “Provide simple answers to simple questions.”

Readability analysis

19 years of formal education required to comprehend key pages

A formal readability analysis of key pages (including eligibility information) confirmed the user feedback. The text required approximately 19 years of formal education to comprehend—equivalent to several years of post-secondary education. This created a significant barrier for the broad population the site was intended to serve.

Recommendations

I delivered a clear, prioritized set of recommendations focused on visibility, simplicity, and inclusivity:

  1. Redesign the primary CTA(s) with conventional button styling, strong contrast, hover states, and higher placement on the page (e.g., overlaying part of the hero area). Include brief scope notes explaining each pathway.
  2. Test variations of CTA wording (e.g., common user terms like “enroll” vs. site language).
  3. Simplify the megamenu by consolidating categories and reducing depth.
  4. Add pagination and “items per page” controls at both the top and bottom of resource lists.

  5. Systematically simplify on-page language to plain-language standards appropriate for the general public, using readability tools for validation and iteration.

    How to apply this recommendation

    • Simplify all public-facing content so it is appropriate for a general audience rather than subject-matter experts.
    • Target a 4th- to 7th-grade reading level for maximum accessibility, using established online readability tools to guide sentence structure, word choice, and overall clarity.
    • Prioritize plain-language principles: shorter sentences, common words, clear structure, and direct answers to common user questions.

    These changes would reduce cognitive load, improve task success, and better support users with varying levels of health literacy and formal education.

Proposed Design Directions (Portfolio Visuals)

Since original materials are confidential, I created new conceptual mockups and artifacts reflecting the recommended improvements:

Interactive prototype

Progressive Navigation Disclosure

This prototype links four screens through a clickable navigation element and demonstrates progressively disclosing one section of the menu at a time. Revealing categories on demand reduces information overload, visual clutter, and link density—so users can focus on a single task path (for example, coverage vs. benefits management) instead of scanning dozens of peer links at once. That approach also shortens decision time, supports people under stress or lower health literacy, and keeps high-priority destinations reachable without competing with secondary content. Click any of the four top-level items in the Resources menu to move between sections and compare each focused state.

Outcomes & Impact

The analysis equipped the teams with evidence-based priorities for reducing friction in coverage-seeking journeys. Recommendations aligned with broader goals around equitable access, health literacy, and user-centered government digital services. Several suggestions (improved CTA visibility, navigation simplification, and plain-language efforts) were directly actionable for enhancing outcomes for the target audience. The client expressed strong interest in implementing the recommendations and was very pleased with the findings and proposed next steps.

Reflections & Key Learnings

This project highlighted critical principles for healthcare-adjacent and public-sector UX:

  1. 01

    Behavioral data is irreplaceable

    for uncovering hidden friction points.

  2. 02

    Health literacy is foundational accessibility

    Complex language creates real barriers to care.

  3. 03

    Small design details scale dramatically

    in high-intent, high-stakes flows.

  4. 04

    Designing for public benefits

    reinforced the human impact of UX decisions on vulnerable populations.

I apply these lessons ongoing in my health education work and healthcare UX portfolio projects, emphasizing plain language, equitable design, and systems that support users under cognitive or emotional load.
